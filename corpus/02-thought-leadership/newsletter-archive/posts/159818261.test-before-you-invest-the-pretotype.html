The Validation Trinity

The product development process has evolved far beyond the old "build it and they will come" mentality.

Today's strategic product leaders understand the crucial differences between:

  1. Pretotyping: Market validation with minimal investment. ("Would anyone care?")

  2. Prototyping: Experience validation with simulated functionality. ("Can users figure it out?")

  3. MVP: Solution validation with core features only. ("Does this solve the problem?")

Each represents a progressive investment level, designed to protect you from the most expensive mistake in product development: building something nobody wants, perfectly.

The Problem with Skipping Steps

The data behind our industry's tendency to jump straight to building is sobering:

  • 54% of roadmaps are designed around outputs (features) rather than outcomes

  • Product managers spend over half their time firefighting instead of building strategically

  • Teams with high psychological safety outperform others by 20%, yet many fear proposing experimental approaches

In my own experience, I've seen the transformative power of starting small. At a previous organization, I helped break the mold of big-bang delivery by implementing a lightweight solution that initially seemed too simple to succeed.

What began as a modest experiment evolved through iterations based on actual user feedback, eventually becoming a core offering for our entire customer base.

The lesson - Starting small isn't just fiscally responsible - it's strategically brilliant. You learn what customers actually want before building what you think they need.

Pretotyping: The Art of Fake It Before You Make It

Pretotyping is the art of market validation without engineering involvement - the product world's equivalent of asking someone on a coffee date before proposing marriage.

Dropbox's legendary explainer video increased their beta waitlist from 5,000 to 75,000 overnight - all before writing a single line of code. The cost? A few days of video production versus months of engineering.

Effective pretotyping approaches include:

  • The Concierge Method: Manually deliver your service to early customers (CarsDirect sold cars before having systems to process orders)

  • The Fake Door: Create marketing materials and sign-up flows for nonexistent features to gauge interest

  • The Impersonator: Use existing tools to simulate your product experience (Zapier connecting multiple services to mimic automation)

Moving Through the Validation Chain

Once your pretotype confirms genuine interest (or saves you from the special kind of despair that comes from building a feature nobody asked for), it's time to graduate through the validation hierarchy with the careful precision of someone carrying coffee through a laptop-filled room.

Prototype Stage: Here's where you create interactive experiences that test usability without committing to backend systems—like dating someone before meeting their parents. Companies embracing accessible design principles at this stage have seen user satisfaction increase by 20%, a metric that makes both users and executives remarkably happy.

MVP Development: This is where you build only what's necessary to deliver core value—the product development equivalent of packing for a trip with only a carry-on. Organizations taking this disciplined, product-led growth approach have experienced 30% higher customer retention compared to those throwing features at the wall to see what sticks.

So how do we actually put this validation sequence into practice without causing your team to roll their eyes at "yet another process change"?

Let's walk through the logical progression that turns skeptical teams into pretotyping evangelists faster than you can say "we should have tested that first."

Implementing a Validation Culture

To incorporate this strategic sequence in your organization:

  1. Start with the riskiest assumption - What belief, if wrong, would torpedo your entire concept?

  2. Design a minimalist test - What's the absolute simplest way to validate or invalidate that assumption?

  3. Establish clear thresholds - Define what success looks like before running the test

  4. Graduate through stages - Only invest in prototyping after pretotype validation, and MVP after prototype validation

I've implemented this approach with multiple teams, and the most common response is: "I can't believe we've been skipping the pretotyping stage all this time."

Your Validation Challenge

This week, take your most promising backlog item and ask: "How could we validate this with a pretotype before involving engineering?"

Start with this OG format: "We believe [target users] will [specific action] to achieve [desired outcome]."

Then design your lightest possible test. Your engineers will appreciate not building features destined for the product graveyard, and your metrics will reflect the improved focus on validated value.

Remember: In product development, knowing what not to build is often more valuable than knowing how to build everything perfectly.
